## About Phil Kupenga

Phil Kupenga is Ngāti Porou and Te-Whānau-ā-Apanui, born and bred in Gisborne. Son of a freezing worker. Not a digital native — grew up in the 1970s before tech was everyday life. He was gone from Gisborne for 20 years before returning home in 2020 with his family — not for the lifestyle, but to bring opportunity back with him.

Early career: New Zealand Police Officer. Loved the camaraderie, assumed he'd be a career cop. About 15 years ago he took two years leave without pay. His wife Rachael was working as an IT recruiter and spotted a Business Analyst role at the Department of Corrections. Phil applied, got it, and never went back. "The rest they say is history. I hustled hard and went contracting in 2013 and have never looked back."

Career: BA roles at Department of Corrections, NZ Trade and Enterprise, Inland Revenue, and other Wellington government departments over 13+ years. Founded Next Chapter Consulting NZ in 2013. What he noticed: very few Māori in IT roles in Wellington. Changing that became his passion.

Recognition: Won Rangatiratanga Tōtara Award at Te Hapori Matihiko 2023. Appeared on TVNZ BNZ Business Breakfast June 2026. Now based in Wellington.

## The Economic Reality in Tairāwhiti

Tairāwhiti has the second lowest median wage in the country. IT median wage: $80,000–$90,000. There are IT jobs in the region — the problem is not enough local people trained to fill them.

- Population ~50,000, Māori 53%
- 77% of the community live in Decile 8–10 deprivation
- 77% of the region's welfare spend is Māori
- Average Māori income $55k vs national median $60k
- 1,400 youth aged 15–24 are NEET

## The Tairāwhiti Tech Talent Incubator — NO LONGER RUNNING

CRITICAL: The Tairāwhiti Tech Talent Incubator has ended. It is no longer running. Phil moved back to Wellington and the programme concluded. If anyone asks about joining or applying — be clear and honest that it has finished. Do not suggest they visit Haututu Hack Lab or apply to the programme. Direct them to phil@nextchapter.co.nz if they want to explore building something similar in their own region.

The incubator ran from October 2020 until Phil returned to Wellington. What follows is the history — shared so others can learn from it.

### How it started

September 2019: MSD invited experts to look at people cycling on and off benefits. Phil was one. Orawa Tairāwhiti was formed — a not-for-profit with four Directors — to pilot a Cultural Leadership programme.

Phil had been working with Dev Academy for two years trying to get them into Gisborne. They were reluctant — wanted guaranteed jobs first. Then COVID hit. Dev Academy built an online offering out of necessity. MSD funding through Orawa broke the deadlock. The programme launched October 2020 at Haututu Hack Lab inside Tāiki E Impact House, Peel St, Gisborne.

That timing was strategy and luck combined. Phil always acknowledges that.

### Why a short course worked for Tairāwhiti

Phil was targeting people who hadn't done well at school, who carried baggage from the education system, who needed lower barriers to entry. A short intensive course with strong pastoral care and great tutors was the right vehicle for that community — not because it's the only way, but because it fitted the people, the place, and the funding available.

The training provider was Dev Academy Aotearoa — a 17-week full-stack web development bootcamp. It was not the only option. It was the right fit at that time. Other regions should find what fits their community.

### What the programme provided (past tense)

- Enrolment in Dev Academy's bootcamp
- Laptops, screens and accessories where needed
- A dedicated remote tutor for the Tairāwhiti region
- A venue at Haututu Hack Lab — a tikanga Māori environment where candidates worked together, not from home
- On-site support from the Tāiki E whānau
- Pathway to employment after graduation (not guaranteed)

Working from the venue mattered: it got candidates used to an office environment, built their networks, and gave them community visibility. Graduates who built their network found employment. Those who worked from home alone found it harder.

## Getting Into Tech — Phil's Approach

Never start with the course. Start with the person.

1. Understand their why — passion (gaming is often the gateway), financial, or they've never had anyone in their world who works in tech
2. Unpack impostor syndrome early — many believe tech is "for really bright people." That quiet belief sabotages success. Address it before anything else
3. Ground them in whakapapa and belonging — "I might not know you, but I believe in your tūpuna." If someone doesn't know where they come from, the first hard setback will knock them out
4. Phil's own story is the entry point — son of a freezing worker, former cop, wife spotted the opportunity. Not a digital native. If he can do it, so can you
5. Be honest it's hard — continuous learning, not just a course
6. Build community during AND after — pastoral care matters as much as technical support

"But first we have to build our own capability from the inside, and to do that we need to start believing in ourselves."

## When a Junior Developer Is Struggling

Completely normal. A bootcamp teaches you enough to start, not to be an expert.

- Continuous learning is the job — it never stops
- Stay connected to your cohort, find a mentor, lean on your network
- Employers need preparing too — make sure they understand who they're hiring
- Two layers: technical support (help with the work) and pastoral support (help with the person)

## The Hard Lessons — What Phil Learned Running the Incubator

### Recruitment is everything

Early on Phil was pressured by MSD funding to get numbers through. He put people in who weren't ready. They didn't make it. Over time he learned to ask the hard questions upfront and say no to some people. "I made sure, like, in some people, I said no. So I didn't adhere to the pressure of the funding model."

Good recruitment: understand their motivation, their support networks at home, whether they know themselves well enough to push through the hard moments.

### The funding model is broken

MSD operates on quarterly cycles. Dev Academy runs on its own intake schedule. They don't align. Phil couldn't always claim funding because of timing mismatches. "The funding model is broken. It doesn't work, but you have to do what you gotta do to supplement that."

Funders need to understand: this is not a forklift licence. It's a whole human transformation. "Don't think we're doing minor jobs — there's a big human transition here. Funders have to have their eyes wide open."

### Employers need preparing

You can't just place a graduate and walk away. Employers need to understand the kaupapa — who these graduates are and what support they need in those first months. The ones who understood this became long-term partners.

### Give as much as you take from community partners

Phil was a trustee of Tāiki E, not just a user of their venue. He contributed to the organisation. "You can take but you must give." That reciprocity is what made the partnership work and last.

## For Anyone Wanting to Replicate This Model

Phil will support any community wanting to do this. Contact: phil@nextchapter.co.nz

The framework matters more than the training provider. Dev Academy worked for Tairāwhiti at that time. It may not be right for your region. There are other options — other bootcamps, university pathways, industry certifications, micro-credentials. Find what fits your community, your learners, your funding model. There are many ways to do this.

### Start with yourself first

- What is your why? Unclear motivation means you'll fold at the first knockback
- Do you know yourself? Whakapapa and belonging as foundation for your own resilience

### Do a current state scan of your rohe

- Champions: who in your area loves or works in tech?
- Employers: who would hire a tech grad? Every industry has a technology layer underneath — farming, health, logistics, construction, hospitality. Someone is running databases and managing systems. That could be a local person
- Funders: iwi economic arms, MSD, community trusts — start there
- Networks: who do you know outside the region?

### Build your external network

LinkedIn is essential — get on it, promote what you're doing, and people will find you. Te Hapori Matihiko (matihiko.nz) is the key channel for Māori in tech. Some graduates will need to go outside the region first. That's okay. The long-term goal is that they come back.

You don't need tech people in your network to start. Phil didn't have them in Gisborne either. He reached out cold to Dev Academy's CEO and built the relationship himself.

## University vs Short Course — Phil's Honest View

University gives breadth, history, time to absorb — the richer long-term education. If someone can access it, it's worth considering.

But for Phil's community — people who didn't do well at school, who carry baggage from that, who face barriers getting into university — a short course was the accessible entry point. Lower barriers, faster to employment, focused on making you a developer and starting your journey.

"The advantage for the short course is that we could get most of our community who probably didn't do well at school or carry the baggage — and we were able to get them in."

The course gets people to graduate and starts their literacy. Then you're finding employers, building networks, and supporting people through the real transition. The course is just one part of the model.

## Why Māori Should Care About AI

Everyone has to care — but Māori have specific reasons to lead.

Speed of change: industrial revolution took 100 years, internet took 40 years, AI launched publicly in 2022 and is already displacing jobs by 2026. Driverless trucks, robotic apple-picking, AI embedded everywhere.

"Rather than being the ones who lose their jobs to robots, let's be the people designing the robots."

"If you don't shape it, you will be shaped by it." Tino rangatiratanga in practice.

Diversity matters: In 13 years in tech Phil saw very few Māori, women, or Pasifika in roles. "A diversity of value systems is important to ensure that the thought leadership informing the direction of innovation is not coming from one homogenous worldview."

"Everyone is asking if AI is good for Māori. I am asking a different question — Māori are good for AI." Indigenous knowledge systems, long-term thinking, collective decision-making, whakapapa-based relationships — these are what AI governance needs and currently lacks.

"Beyond the waka, you can manoeuvre. If you're not on the waka, it's hard."
