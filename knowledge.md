## Getting Into Tech — Phil's Approach

Never start with the course. Start with the person.

1. Understand their why — passion, financial, or never had anyone in their world in tech
2. Unpack impostor syndrome — many believe tech is for really bright people. Address this first
3. Ground in whakapapa — "I might not know you but I believe in your tupuna"
4. Phil's story is the entry point — son of a freezing worker, former cop, wife spotted the opportunity
5. Be honest it is hard — continuous learning not just a course
6. Build community during AND after — pastoral care matters as much as technical support

"But first we have to build our own capability from the inside, and to do that we need to start believing in ourselves."

## Hard Lessons from Running the Incubator

Recruitment is everything: Early on Phil took numbers because MSD funding pressured him to. People who were not ready did not make it. Over time he learned to say no. "I made sure, like, in some people, I said no. So I did not adhere to the pressure of the funding model."

The funding model is broken: MSD quarterly cycles and Dev Academy intakes do not align. "The funding model is broken. It does not work, but you have to do what you gotta do to supplement that." Funders need to understand this is a whole human transformation — not a forklift licence.

Employers need preparing: Cannot just place a graduate and walk away. Employers must understand the kaupapa.

Give as much as you take: Phil was a trustee of Tāiki E, not just a user of their venue. "You can take but you must give."

The framework matters more than the training provider. Dev Academy worked for Tairāwhiti at that time. Find what fits your community. There are many ways to do this.

## Feel It. Reframe It. Do It. — Phil's AI Readiness Framework

This is Phil's three-part framework for how individuals move through AI change.

Feel It: Be human first. Any change triggers real emotions — identity, fear, uncertainty. You have to actually feel that and work through it before you can grasp the change. Most people want to jump straight to tools. Until you have done the emotional and identity work, you will not actually be ready to move.

Reframe It: AI is not a rupture — it is a continuation. Internet built the infrastructure and connected the world. Social media built attention and behaviour data. Smartphones put access in everyone's pocket. AI is the next wave — and it could only exist because all that infrastructure came first. If you use an iPhone, you are already in it. If you have posted on social media, you are already in it.

The torch analogy: Phil does not know exactly where AI is going. Nobody does. Like walking with a torch in the dark — you know the general direction, and when something changes you pivot. Still have a hundred-year vision. But be genuinely agile. Most people and organisations are not actually built for that kind of adaptability. That is the honest truth.

Do It: Start using AI. ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini — pick one and use it every day. Learn to prompt. Make it your assistant. You only really learn by doing. Then go further and build with it. That is where the real learning happens.

The grounding reminder: Technology is just a tool. It has democratised things that used to require huge budgets. You can now build a business from a remote location that competes with large organisations. Stay open minded. Know what matters most — for Phil that is people, whanau, being human.

## Compression — Phil's AI Thesis

No profession will disappear — but every profession will compress. AI will augment roles, automate background work, and reduce the number of people needed to do the same volume of work. A law firm with fifty lawyers may operate with five. A marketing team of twenty may become four people with AI agents doing execution.

What compression means for individuals: your role will change. What remains — what AI cannot replicate — is operational judgment, human instinct, reading a room, building trust, selling a concept, showing resilience. Those are the things to invest in.

What compression means for organisations: old leadership hierarchies may not be fit for purpose in a compressed world. Leaders need to think now about what organisational patterns will work, not assume the old ones transfer.

The honest caveat: there are real debates about AI costs and power consumption that may change the economics. Go in with eyes wide open.

## Character Over Career — What Matters in an AI World

Stop thinking about skills. Think about character.

AI cannot sell a concept. It cannot read a room. It cannot build genuine trust. It cannot show resilience after failure. It cannot exercise operational judgment in the moment. These become more valuable as AI handles more technical and repeatable work.

For people already in careers: dig into the human side of your role. What does your role require that AI genuinely cannot replicate? That is where to invest.

For young people: do not let anyone tell you which specific career to aim for — nobody knows what jobs will look like in ten years. Build character instead:
- Resilience — the ability to fail, get up, and keep moving
- Critical thinking — questioning and analysing, not just accepting
- Adaptability — genuinely comfortable with change
- Relationships and social capital — trust takes time and effort, matters more in an AI world
- Ability to sell a story and persuade people
- Entrepreneurial thinking — knowing the problem, convincing others

Sport and arts matter more than people realise. Sport teaches competition, failure, resilience, teamwork, and networks. Arts and creativity build imaginative capabilities AI cannot replicate. These are not soft extras — they are core preparation for an AI world.

Networks matter more than ever. In a compressed world there are fewer spots. Who you know and who trusts you is a real competitive advantage.

## The Economic Levelling — Small Teams High Margins

AI has fundamentally levelled the economic playing field. A small consultancy — even a one-person operation — can now compete with medium and large organisations. Things that used to require big budgets (media, marketing, research, content, administration, analysis) can now be done by a small team with AI agents doing execution.

Phil's own model: low overheads, high margins. In the old world that was hard to scale because you needed more people in more locations. In the AI world agents work 24/7, handle repeatable work, and keep costs down. AI was the missing piece for Phil — as someone who has always believed in lean high-value operation.

The Sam Altman idea: we will see the first one-person billion-dollar companies. Small agile teams with AI agents can produce what previously required large organisations. Five-person companies outperforming hundred-person companies is not far away.

The honest debate: AI costs and power consumption at scale may shift the economics. The low-cost AI environment today may not last. Factor this into planning.

## AI-Ready Organisations — What Leaders Should Do

The structural question first: if compression happens and you have smaller teams doing the same work, do your current leadership patterns still work? A hierarchy built for five hundred people may not serve fifty. Leaders need to think now about what organisational structures fit an AI-compressed world.

What an AI-ready leader does:

1. Upskill yourself first. If you do not have genuine digital literacy and AI understanding, start there. You cannot lead your organisation through a change you do not understand yourself.

2. Look at your people honestly. How will this affect them? What does the transition look like? How do you manage it responsibly?

3. Review processes and procedures. Where is AI already being used or could be? Where are the risks — hallucination, use of private data, decisions made without human oversight?

4. Build AI governance. If your people are using AI tools, do you have guardrails? Human oversight? Clear policies on what data can and cannot go into AI systems? Phil's product Tupuna Pai (tupunapai.com) is built specifically around this.

5. Accept the change before trying to manage it. Leaders who are waiting for certainty before acting are already behind.

Do a current state analysis first: understand where your organisation actually is — not where you think it is — in terms of digital literacy, AI awareness, and readiness for change. This is core to what Phil and Te Amokura Consultants do with organisations.

## Identity as Foundation — Being More Human in an AI World

Being more human in an AI world starts with knowing who you are and what you stand for.

For Maori, this is pepeha and whakapapa — knowing where you come from gives you a place to stand when everything around you is changing. That is not a soft idea. That is infrastructure.

For all workers, identity is the foundation that holds when roles compress and skills become automated. The people who will navigate AI disruption best are not just the most skilled — they are the ones with the clearest sense of self.

When someone is worried their role is being disrupted or replaced, remind them: AI can compress their skills but it cannot touch who they are, where they come from, or what they stand for. That is theirs. No algorithm can take it.

This is why Phil always starts with the person before the course, the tool, or the plan. Why he asks about whakapapa before asking about career goals. Why the Feel it. Reframe it. Do it. framework starts with feeling — not doing. You cannot move well in a changing world if you do not know who you are in it.

For roles grounded in community, culture, relationships, and service — this matters even more. A nurse, a teacher, a community worker, a kaitiaki — AI will change how they work but it cannot replace what they bring. The human layer in those roles is not a nice-to-have. It is the whole point.

Identity is not the soft stuff. It is the thing that holds everything else together.

## Phil Kupenga Indigenous Delivery Framework

This is the framework Phil developed and uses when implementing community change programmes — including the Tairāwhiti Tech Talent Incubator.

Core belief: Everything starts with belief in the people and the community. Begin with trust — in their knowledge, potential, and solutions. This is not theory — it is proven.

The framework has six living stages:

Stage 1 — Listen: Begin with people, not paperwork. Hear their stories, not just their data. Iwi, hapu, whanau, community, stakeholders and organisations all define what matters most. Stories before systems. Outcome: a "what matters most" list — the human brief that guides everything.

Stage 2 — Collaborate: Set the kaupapa and tikanga. Agree on shared values and how decisions are made — the project kawa. Shared tikanga builds trust and safety. Co-create outcomes that are real and adaptive. Success evolves as you learn. Outcome: a 1-page cultural charter and a living outcomes map shaped by people.

Stage 3 — Design: Build the right governance — a joint ropu of leaders who decide fast and adapt fast. Agility comes from clarity. Run short wananga and sprints with real users. Try early, learn fast. Outcome: tested, practical blueprints for change.

Stage 4 — Deliver: Maori and Indigenous professionals lead the mahi, backed by assurance and data. Action plus reflection equals momentum. Start small, measure, refine, expand. Each pilot is a learning lab. Outcome: proven solutions ready to scale.

Stage 5 — Reflect: Keep everyone informed, connected, and involved. Transparency builds trust. Track both KPIs and human indicators — wellbeing, inclusion, mauri. We value both efficiency and humanity. Outcome: dashboards that show impact, not just activity.

Stage 6 — Strengthen: Transfer capability — train, buddy, and empower internal teams to sustain delivery. Grow capability, not dependence. Hold wananga ako every 4-6 weeks to reflect and adapt. 3-, 6-, and 12-month check-ins ensure outcomes endure. Accountability is relationship, not reporting. Outcome: local, self-determined delivery.

How the Tairāwhiti incubator applied this framework:
- Listen: Sat with community to understand their aspirations
- Collaborate: Partnered with MSD, community, and the tech industry
- Design: Co-designed a Maori-led training pathway with real users
- Deliver: Built and ran the programme — Maori professionals led the mahi
- Reflect: Tracked both employment outcomes and human wellbeing
- Strengthen: Transferred capability so the model could endure and be replicated

Outcome: Participants moved from benefits to tech careers. 90% still employed three years later. A real example of belief and collective strength turning kaupapa into lasting change.

The philosophy behind it: Ko te ao hurihuri — the world keeps turning. This framework is a living system for transformation. Built on kaupapa. Strengthened by learning. Measured in trust. The goal is not to build projects — it is to deliver outcomes, grow people, relationships, and futures.
