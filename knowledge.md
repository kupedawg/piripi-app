## About Phil Kupenga

Phil Kupenga is Ngāti Porou and Te-Whānau-ā-Apanui, born and bred in Gisborne. Son of a freezing worker. Not a digital native — grew up in the 1970s before tech was everyday life. He was gone from Gisborne for 20 years before returning home in 2020 with his family — not for the lifestyle, but to bring opportunity back with him.

Early career: New Zealand Police Officer. Loved the camaraderie, assumed he'd be a career cop. About 15 years ago he took two years leave without pay. His wife Rachael was working as an IT recruiter and spotted a Business Analyst role at the Department of Corrections. Phil applied, got it, and never went back to the police. "The rest they say is history. I hustled hard and went contracting in 2013 and have never looked back."

Career: BA roles at Department of Corrections, NZ Trade and Enterprise, Inland Revenue, and other Wellington government departments over 13+ years. Founded Next Chapter Consulting NZ in 2013.

What he noticed: Very few Māori in IT roles in Wellington. Changing that became his passion.

Recognition: Won Rangatiratanga Tōtara Award at Te Hapori Matihiko 2023. Appeared on TVNZ BNZ Business Breakfast June 2026.

## The Economic Reality in Tairāwhiti

Tairāwhiti has the second lowest median wage in the country. IT median wage: $80,000–$90,000. There are IT jobs in the region — the problem is not enough local people trained to fill them.

- Population ~50,000, Māori 53%
- 77% of the community live in Decile 8–10 deprivation
- 77% of the region's welfare spend is Māori
- Average Māori income $55k vs national median $60k
- 1,400 youth aged 15–24 are NEET (Not in Employment, Education or Training)

## How the Incubator Started

September 2019: MSD invited experts to look at people cycling on and off benefits. Phil was one. Orawa Tairāwhiti was formed — a not-for-profit with four Directors — to pilot a Cultural Leadership programme. Phil's role: inspire people toward high-value work in tech and entrepreneurship.

Phil spotted the Ka Hao fund (for Māori in tech), applied, didn't get it. Noticed Dev Academy was successful and offering Māori scholarships. Reached out directly to Dev Academy CEO Rohan Wakefield about setting up in Gisborne. Dev Academy was reluctant — wanted guaranteed jobs first (chicken and egg). MSD funding through Orawa solved it. By 2020 Dev Academy had an online offering and the partnership was struck.

Programme launched October 2020. Has since moved off MSD funding, now run by Dev Academy and Tāiki E partners directly.

## Getting Into Tech — Phil's Approach

Never start with the course. Start with the person.

1. Understand their why — passion (gaming is often the gateway), financial, or they've never had anyone in their world who works in tech.
2. Unpack impostor syndrome early — many believe tech is "for really bright people." That quiet belief sabotages success. Address it before the course starts.
3. Ground them in whakapapa and belonging — "I might not know you, but I believe in your tūpuna." If someone doesn't know where they come from, the first hard setback will knock them out.
4. Phil's own story is the entry point — son of a freezing worker, former cop, wife spotted the opportunity. Not a digital native. If he can do it, so can you.
5. Be honest it's hard — full-time is 60–70 hours a week at peak. Continuous learning, not just a course.
6. Build community — during AND after. Pastoral care matters as much as technical support.

"But first we have to build our own capability from the inside, and to do that we need to start believing in ourselves."

## When a Junior Developer Is Struggling

Completely normal. A bootcamp teaches you enough to start, not to be an expert.

- Continuous learning is the job — it never stops
- Stay connected to your cohort, find a mentor, lean on your network
- Employers need preparing too — make sure they understand who they're hiring and what support is needed
- Two layers needed: technical (help with the work) and pastoral (help with the person)

## The Hard Lessons — What Phil Learned

### Recruitment is everything

Early on Phil was pressured by MSD funding to get numbers through. He put people in who weren't ready. They didn't make it. Over time he learned to ask the hard questions upfront, understand a person's why and support networks, and say no to some people. "I made sure, like, in some people, I said no. So I didn't adhere to the pressure of the funding model."

Good recruitment means: understand their motivation, their support networks at home, whether they know themselves well enough to push through when it gets hard.

### The funding model is broken

MSD operates on quarterly cycles. Dev Academy runs on its own intake schedule. They don't always line up. Phil couldn't always claim funding because of timing mismatches. "The funding model is broken. It doesn't work, but you have to do what you gotta do to supplement that."

Funders need to understand: this is not a forklift licence. It's a whole human transformation. "Don't think we're doing minor jobs — there's a big human transition here. Funders have to have their eyes wide open."

### Employers need preparing

You can't just place a graduate and walk away. Employers need to understand the kaupapa — what the programme is trying to do, who these graduates are, what support they may need in those first months.

## Setting Up in Your Rohe — Starting With Nothing

Yes it's possible. Every region needs to do this. But go in clear-eyed.

Start with yourself:
- What's your why? Unclear motivation means you'll fold at the first knockback
- Do you know yourself? Whakapapa and belonging as foundation

Current state scan:
- Champions: who in your area loves or works in tech?
- Employers: who would hire a tech grad? Even non-tech companies have hidden tech needs
- Funders: iwi economic arms, MSD, community trusts — start there
- Networks: who do you know outside the region?

Key partners to find: a training provider (Dev Academy), a community venue with kaupapa Māori environment (like Tāiki E), and a funder.

You don't need tech people in your network to start. Phil didn't have them in Gisborne either. He reached out cold to Dev Academy's CEO and built the relationship himself.

## University vs Short Course

University gives breadth, history, time to absorb — it's the richer long-term education. If someone can access it, it's worth considering.

But for Phil's community — people who didn't do well at school, who carry baggage from that, who face barriers getting into university — the short course was the accessible entry point. Lower barriers, faster to employment, focused on making you a developer.

"The advantage for the short course is that we could get most of our community who probably didn't do well at school or carry the baggage — and we were able to get them in."

Dev Academy is one model that worked. It's not the only option — look at what training providers suit your context and community.

## Why Māori Should Care About AI

Everyone has to care — but Māori have specific reasons to lead.

Speed of change:
- Industrial revolution (horse to car): 100 years
- Internet: 40 years
- AI: launched publicly 2022, already displacing jobs by 2026

"Rather than being the ones who lose their jobs to robots, let's be the people designing the robots."

"If you don't shape it, you will be shaped by it." Tino rangatiratanga in practice.

Diversity matters: In 13 years in tech Phil saw very few Māori, women, or Pasifika in roles. "A diversity of value systems is important to ensure that the thought leadership informing the direction of innovation is not coming from one homogenous worldview."

"Everyone is asking if AI is good for Māori. I am asking a different question — Māori are good for AI." Indigenous knowledge systems, long-term thinking, collective decision-making, whakapapa-based relationships — these are what AI governance needs.

"Beyond the waka, you can manoeuvre. If you're not on the waka, it's hard."
